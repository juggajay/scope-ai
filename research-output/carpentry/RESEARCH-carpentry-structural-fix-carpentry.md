# Section 4: Structural & Fix Carpentry -- Australian Residential Research

**Research Date:** February 2026
**Purpose:** Comprehensive reference for ScopeAI scope-of-works generation covering structural carpentry, fix carpentry, timber flooring, and decking in Australian residential construction.
**Confidence Note:** This research is compiled from Australian Standards (AS 1684, NCC/BCA), manufacturer specifications, and established industry practice. Specific span tables, engineering requirements, and BAL ratings must always be verified against the current edition of the relevant standard and local council requirements. Pricing is excluded -- this document covers scope items, specifications, and common inclusions only.

## A. Framing / Structural Carpentry

### A1. New Wall Framing (Stud Walls)

#### Scope Inclusions
| Item | Typical Specification | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Bottom plate | 90x45mm or 70x35mm (match stud) | Anchored to slab/floor with masonry anchors or coach screws |
| Top plate | 90x45mm or 70x35mm (match stud) | Double top plate for load-bearing walls |
| Studs | 90x45mm (load-bearing), 70x35mm (non-load-bearing internal) | Kiln-dried MGP10 minimum |
| Stud spacing | 450mm centres (standard), 600mm centres (max for non-load-bearing) | 450mm required where sheet bracing is used |
| Noggings (dwangs) | Same timber as studs | At 1350mm maximum centres per AS 1684 |
| Bracing | Structural ply, metal strap, or proprietary bracing | As per bracing schedule in engineering or AS 1684 |
| Corner and junction studs | Double or triple studs at corners and T-junctions | Required for fixing substrate |
| Trimmer studs | Around openings (doors, windows, hatches) | Paired with jack studs to support lintels |

#### Load-Bearing vs Non-Load-Bearing -- Scope Differences

| Aspect | Load-Bearing | Non-Load-Bearing |
|---|---|---|
| Timber size | 90x45mm MGP10 minimum (often 90x35mm MGP10 per span tables) | 70x35mm MGP10 acceptable for internal partitions |
| Top plate | Double top plate required | Single top plate acceptable |
| Engineering | Must comply with AS 1684 span tables or specific engineering | Generally prescriptive; no specific engineering unless bracing wall |
| Stud spacing | Typically 450mm centres | 450mm or 600mm centres |
| Lintel over openings | Engineered lintel required (sized per span tables) | Lightweight lintel or single header acceptable |
| Bracing requirements | May form part of bracing schedule | May form part of bracing schedule |
| Noggings | 1350mm max centres | 1350mm max centres |

#### Timber Grades

| Grade | Description | Use |
|---|---|---|
| MGP10 | Machine Graded Pine -- minimum structural grade | Standard wall framing, non-load-bearing, general framing |
| MGP12 | Higher bending strength | Load-bearing walls, longer spans, higher loads |
| MGP15 | Premium structural grade | Heavy load-bearing applications, engineered designs |
| F5 / F7 | Stress-graded hardwood equivalents | Older buildings, specific hardwood applications |

**Treatment requirements:**
- H2 (blue) treated pine: Required for all framing in termite-risk areas (most of Australia per AS 3660.1)
- H2-S: Envelope treatment suitable for framing protected from weather
- H3: Required where timber is exposed to weather but not in ground contact
- Untreated: Only where local regulations allow and termite management system is in place

### A2. Wall Removal & Structural Support

#### Scope Items for Wall Removal

| Stage | Scope Item | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-work | Structural engineer engagement | Engineer to assess load paths, specify beam size, connection details, and temporary propping |
| Pre-work | Services identification | Electrical, plumbing, gas, data -- relocate before demolition |
| Temporary works | Temporary propping | Acrow props, needles, strong-boy brackets -- installed both sides of wall |
| Temporary works | Needle beams | Steel beams inserted through wall to transfer loads to temporary props |
| Demolition | Wall removal | Stud wall or masonry removal (different methods) |
| Permanent structure | Steel beam (UB or PFC) | As specified by structural engineer; typically hot-dipped galvanised or primed |
| Permanent structure | LVL beam | Laminated Veneer Lumber alternative to steel, sized by engineer |
| Permanent structure | Posts / columns | Steel SHS/RHS posts or timber posts to support beam ends |
| Permanent structure | Post footings | Concrete pad footings or connection to existing structure per engineer's detail |
| Permanent structure | Connections | Bolted connections, brackets, coach screws -- per engineer's specification |
| Make good | Ceiling and wall patching | Plasterboard, cornice, paint -- typically plasterer's scope |
| Certification | Engineer's inspection | Structural engineer to certify completed work matches approved design |

#### Carpenter's Scope vs Engineer's Scope

| Carpenter Does | Engineer Does |
|---|---|
| Install temporary propping per engineer's direction | Design temporary propping scheme |
| Remove wall framing/lining | Specify beam size, type, connections |
| Install steel or LVL beam | Certify footing design |
| Install posts, brackets, connections | Inspect and certify completed structural work |
| Pack and shim beam to level | Provide PS1 design certificate and PS4 inspection certificate (NSW) |
| Coordinate with other trades for services relocation | Sign off on load path adequacy |

**Common beam types used in Australian residential:**
- UB (Universal Beam): 150UB14, 200UB18, 200UB25, 250UB26, 310UB32
- PFC (Parallel Flange Channel): 150PFC, 200PFC, 250PFC
- SHS (Square Hollow Section): 65x65x3.0, 75x75x3.5, 89x89x3.5 (for posts)
- LVL (Laminated Veneer Lumber): Brands include Wesbeam e-beam, Carter Holt Harvey Futura, CHH HySpan

### A3. Floor Framing Modifications

#### Scope Items

| Modification | What's Involved | Key Specifications |
|---|---|---|
| New floor opening (stairs) | Cut existing joists, install trimmer joists and trimming joists, headers | Trimmers typically doubled; connections per AS 1684 or engineer |
| New floor opening (services) | Smaller openings for plumbing, HVAC | Max hole diameter per AS 1684 tables; reinforcement if oversized |
| Reinforcement for heavy items | Additional joists or blocking under baths, stone benchtops, island benches | Typically 2-3 additional joists or solid blocking; engineer may specify |
| Floor levelling (timber) | Packing, sister joists, or self-levelling compound over existing | Tolerance: 3mm in 1m for tile substrates per AS 3958.1 |
| Floor levelling (concrete slab) | Grinding high spots, self-levelling compound for low spots | Typically 10-50mm pour depth; product-dependent |
| Joist repair/replacement | Sistering new joist alongside damaged member | Same size or larger; bolted or nailed per AS 1684 |

**Common floor joist sizes (residential):**
- 190x45mm (most common for spans to ~3.6m)
- 240x45mm (longer spans to ~4.5m)
- 290x45mm (longer spans to ~5.4m)
- LVL / I-joists for longer spans or engineered solutions (Posi-Struts, Wesbeam)

### A4. Ceiling Framing

#### Scope Items

| Item | Detail | Specification |
|---|---|---|
| New bulkhead framing | Timber or steel frame dropped below ceiling line | Typically 70x35mm or 90x35mm pine; faced with plasterboard |
| Rangehood bulkhead | Encloses ducting from rangehood to external wall/roof | Usually 400-600mm deep x 600-900mm wide; coordinate with kitchen design |

**Ceiling fan fixing requirements:**
- Minimum support capacity: 15kg for standard fans, up to 25kg for larger fans
- Solid timber blocking (minimum 90x45mm) fixed between joists directly above mounting point
- Fan brace or approved metal bracket for retrofit installations
- Must comply with electrical installation standards (AS/NZS 3000)

### A5. Door Opening Modifications

#### Scope Items by Type

| Modification | Scope Inclusions | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Widening existing opening | Remove existing trimmer studs, cut back existing lintel, install new wider lintel, new trimmer/jack studs, make good wall linings | Lintel sizing per AS 1684 span tables or engineer for load-bearing |
| Creating new opening (non-load-bearing) | Mark out, cut studs, install lintel/header, trimmer studs, jack studs | Lintel can be single 90x45mm for openings to ~1200mm in non-load-bearing |
| Creating new opening (load-bearing) | Temporary propping, cut studs, install engineered lintel, trimmer and jack studs | Engineering required; lintel typically LVL, steel angle, or built-up timber |
| Closing off existing opening | Infill framing -- new studs at 450/600mm centres, noggings, bottom plate | Match existing stud size and spacing; plasterboard both sides |
| Converting window to door | Remove window, extend opening to floor level, new lintel if wider, threshold detail | Check if opening width needs increasing (820mm min clear for door) |

**Standard lintel sizes (non-load-bearing):**
- Openings to 1200mm: Single 90x45mm MGP10
- Openings 1200-1800mm: Double 90x45mm MGP10
- Openings 1800-2400mm: Triple 90x45mm or LVL -- check span tables

**Standard lintel sizes (load-bearing):**
- Must be sized per AS 1684 span tables based on roof load width, storey height, and opening span
- Common solutions: LVL beams (e.g., 200x45mm HySpan), steel angle (100x100x6 EA, 150x100x10 UA), built-up timber

| Modification | Scope Inclusions | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|

### B3. Architrave Installation

#### Common Profiles (Australia)

| Profile | Description | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| Colonial | Ogee and step profile; ornate | Federation, Queenslander, traditional homes |
| Bullnose | Simple rounded edge | Versatile; suits most home styles |
| Ovolo | Quarter-round profile | Traditional to transitional homes |
| Pencil round | Small radius on edges only | Modern, clean lines |
| Lambs tongue | Slightly curved/bevelled profile | Heritage and period homes |
| Square edge / Modern | Flat face, minimal profile | Contemporary, minimalist homes |
| Shadow line / Shadowline | Recessed channel at wall-to-frame junction (no architrave) | High-end modern; requires plaster bead detailing |

#### Architrave Dimensions

| Width (mm) | Application | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 42 | Minimal / modern | Sometimes used as window stop bead |
| 66 | Standard / most common | Suits most residential applications |
| 90 | Traditional / feature | Federation and period homes |
| 110+ | Grand / heritage | Double-storey entries, heritage restoration |

**Thickness:** Typically 18mm (standard); 12mm (budget/MDF); 25mm (premium/hardwood).

**Length:** Supplied in 2.4m, 2.7m, 3.0m, 5.4m lengths.

#### Architrave Material Options

| Material | Pros | Cons | Typical Cost Tier |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primed MDF | Smooth finish, no grain, easy to paint, cost-effective | Cannot be stained; swells if wet | Budget |
| Primed pine (finger-jointed) | Paintable, can be stained, good for profiles | Knots may show through paint over time | Mid |
| Hardwood (select grade) | Can be stained or clear-coated; durable | Expensive; harder to work | Premium |
| Raw pine | Cheapest option; requires full priming and painting | Knots, grain; needs more prep | Budget |

#### Square-Set (No Architrave) Detail

Square-set corners eliminate the need for architrave by creating a clean, flush junction between the wall and door frame. This approach requires:

- Plaster bead (shadow-line bead or square-set bead) installed at the junction
- Plastering to a high standard -- any imperfections are visible
- Door jamb must be flush with plasterboard face
- Higher plasterer skill required; typically increases plastering cost by 20-30%
- Popular in modern/contemporary builds
- Shadow-line gap is typically 10mm

#### Installation Methods

| Method | Description | Substrate Required | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secret nail (and glue) | Nails driven at 45 degrees through tongue; adhesive bead on substrate | Timber joists, plywood, or particle board subfloor | Traditional method; most solid timber installations |
| Direct stick (glue-down) | Flexible adhesive applied to substrate; boards laid into adhesive | Concrete slab (must be moisture-tested) or sheet subfloor | Overlay boards; where floor height is a constraint |
| Floating | Boards joined tongue-and-groove; sit on underlay; not fixed to substrate | Any level substrate with foam or rubber underlay | Engineered boards; rental properties; DIY |
| Batten system | Timber battens (treated pine) fixed to slab; boards nailed to battens | Concrete slab | Where slab is uneven; allows services under floor |

**Subfloor requirements:**
- Concrete slab must be moisture tested (per AS 1884): Relative humidity less than 75% (or per adhesive manufacturer)
- Plywood subfloor: Minimum 12mm structural plywood; fixed at 150mm centres to joists
- Particleboard subfloor: Minimum 19mm; must be dry and structurally sound
- Acclimatisation: Boards must acclimatise to site conditions for minimum 7-14 days before installation

### C3. Sanding and Polishing Existing Timber Floors

#### Process Steps

| Step | Detail | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Preparation | Remove furniture; check for nails/screws (punch below surface); repair damaged boards | Any protruding fasteners will tear sanding belts |
| 2. Coarse sand | 36-40 grit belt sander on drum sander; diagonal to grain | Removes old finish, levels boards; edger for perimeters |
| 3. Medium sand | 60-80 grit | Removes scratches from coarse sand |
| 4. Fine sand | 100-120 grit | Smooth finish; ready for coating |
| 5. Gap filling | Timber filler or resin filler mixed with sanding dust | Fill gaps, nail holes, cracks; sand smooth after drying |
| 6. Stain (optional) | Water-based or solvent-based timber stain | Applied between final sand and first coat; test on off-cut |
| 7. First coat (sealer) | Polyurethane sealer coat | Penetrates and seals timber; light sand (220 grit) between coats |
| 8. Second coat | Polyurethane or finish coat | Light sand (220 grit) between coats |
| 9. Third coat (final) | Polyurethane final coat | No sanding after final coat; allow full cure time |

#### Finish Comparison

| Feature | Water-Based Polyurethane | Oil-Based (Solvent) Polyurethane | Hardwax Oil |
|---|---|---|---|
| Appearance | Clear, non-yellowing | Amber/warm tone; yellows over time | Natural, matte, low-sheen |
| Dry time between coats | 2-4 hours | 8-12 hours | 8-24 hours |
| Full cure time | 7-14 days | 21-30 days | 7-14 days |
| Coats required | 3 coats minimum | 3 coats minimum | 2-3 coats |
| Odour | Low VOC; minimal odour | High VOC; strong odour during application | Low-moderate VOC |
| Durability | Good; improving with modern formulations | Excellent; traditional choice for high-traffic | Good; repairable (spot repair possible) |
| Sheen options | Matt, satin, semi-gloss, gloss | Matt, satin, semi-gloss, gloss | Matt, satin |
| Walk-on time | 24 hours after final coat | 48-72 hours after final coat | 24-48 hours after final coat |
| Furniture placement | 7 days | 14-30 days | 7 days |
| Recoat / maintenance | Full sand and recoat | Full sand and recoat | Spot sand and re-oil (easier maintenance) |

**Common polyurethane brands (Australian market):**
- Water-based: Bona Traffic HD, Bona Mega, Polycure, Loba 2K Supra
- Oil-based: Synteko Classic, Bona Mega (solvent version), Feast Watson Floorseal

- H3: Above-ground, exposed to weather, not in ground contact (decking boards, handrails, fascias)
